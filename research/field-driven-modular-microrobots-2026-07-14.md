## The headline correction (adversarial): they are FLUID-bound, not open-air
The scorecard gave micro-robots **partial (0.5)** credit on open-air operation. This dig shows that was, if anything, **generous**: magnetic/acoustic microrobot swarms operate **predominantly in fluid/liquid media** (plus some air-water-interface + acoustic-bubble work). **No free-air room-scale operation like utility fog requires.** So the open-air gate is a **near-universal microscale wall** — both DNA nanotech (wet buffer) *and* the micro-robot leader are liquid-bound. This **strengthens** the overall verdict: no microscale programmable-matter candidate is close to literal utility fog.

Where micro-robots genuinely lead (and why they still top the microscale ranking): **fast, reversible, multi-mode reconfiguration** driven by external fields.

## What this means for the program
- **Micro-robots keep the microscale foglet-fidelity lead** on reversible reconfiguration + fast field actuation + discrete units + cooperative manipulation — genuinely strong.
- **But the "air-operable" advantage over DNA is smaller than the scorecard implied**: they are fluid-bound too. The honest gap between the microscale leader and utility fog is **operating medium (needs a liquid) + autonomy (externally field-driven, not self-powered) + scale (dozens–thousands, not trillions)**.
- **Net verdict (reinforced):** no microscale candidate is close to utility fog; the two "closest" (DNA, micro-robots) are BOTH liquid-bound, differing mainly in whether reconfiguration is chemical (DNA, slow) or field-driven (micro-robots, fast).
